What is a Framework?

A framework is a structured approach or set of guidelines that helps organize and streamline the process of solving problems or building projects.

  • Provides reusable components and best practices
  • Reduces repetitive work
  • Ensures consistency across projects

5-Step Data Story Framework

A simple framework to structure data-driven stories:

  • Context: Set the stage and explain the background.
  • Data: Present the data that supports your story.
  • Insight: Highlight key findings or patterns.
  • Narrative: Craft a compelling story around the insights.
  • Action: Suggest concrete next steps or recommendations.

Step 1: Context

Set the stage for your data story. Explain what is happening and why it matters.

  • Bus speeds in NYC affect commute times, reliability, and rider satisfaction.
  • City planners and transit agencies monitor speeds to improve service quality.
  • Context: Understanding which routes slowed down can inform operational decisions.

Step 2: Data

Present the relevant data that supports the story.

  • Daily average speed per bus route from 2023–2025.
  • Data broken down by hour, day of week, and borough.
  • Sources: MTA open data, traffic sensors, historical travel times.

Step 3: Insight

Highlight the key findings or patterns in the data.

  • Certain routes in Manhattan and Brooklyn have slowed down significantly in 2025.
  • Peak hours experienced larger slowdowns than off-peak hours.
  • Some routes in outer boroughs maintained consistent speeds.

Step 4: Narrative

Craft a compelling story around the insights.

  • Slowdowns on key routes highlight potential traffic bottlenecks or operational challenges.
  • Commuters in certain areas are disproportionately affected during peak times.
  • Sharing this story can guide targeted improvements and policy decisions.

Step 5: Action

Suggest concrete next steps or recommendations based on your story.

  • Investigate causes of slowdowns on impacted routes (traffic, construction, congestion).
  • Adjust bus schedules or add express services to maintain reliability.
  • Monitor speed trends over time to measure effectiveness of interventions.

Data Workflow: Iterative Process

A robust data workflow is key to uncovering insights and building truth into our data story. It is an iterative process:

  • Ask interesting questions
  • Get the data
  • Explore the data
  • Model the data
  • Visualize the data

Each step informs the next, and repeated iteration ensures accuracy, context, and meaningful insights.

Storytelling Lenses & Ethical Framing

When telling data-driven stories, the perspective or "lens" you choose can shape interpretation. Make sure your story is supported by the data and highlights meaningful impacts.

  • Working-Class Lens: Focus on how bus speed changes affect daily commuters, access to jobs, and quality of life.
  • Operational Lens: Examine the MTA's internal challenges, resource allocation, and scheduling strategies.
  • Policy Lens: Highlight implications for city planning, traffic management, and transit equity.
  • Ethical Storytelling: Always let the data drive your story. Avoid exaggeration; be transparent about limitations and uncertainty.

Applying multiple lenses can make your story richer and more relevant to different audiences.

Frameworks for Telling Stories

There are multiple storytelling frameworks that help structure narratives. While their approaches differ, the underlying principles remain consistent.

  • AND, BUT, THEREFORE: A simple framework for causal storytelling—show the situation, the challenge, and the resolution.
  • Pyramid Principle: Start with the key conclusion, then support it with logically structured arguments and evidence.
  • Subsets & Variations: Many frameworks exist (e.g., 5-step data story, STAR method), but they all emphasize clear context, insight, and action.

Keys that remain the same across frameworks:

  • Context / Situation: Set the stage
  • Insight / Complication: Highlight key findings or challenges
  • Narrative / Resolution: Craft a coherent story
  • Action / Recommendation: Suggest next steps

Creating Tension in Stories

Good stories don't just list events ("AND, AND, AND"). They create tension and keep the audience engaged.

  • BUT: Introduce a complication or challenge that interrupts expectations.
  • THEREFORE: Show the consequence or action that follows from that challenge.

Example (ACE Program & Bus Speeds):

  • The ACE program improved certain routes' bus speeds, BUT some routes still experienced delays during peak hours.
  • THEREFORE, the MTA adjusted schedules, monitored wait times, and optimized stop performance to maintain overall reliability.

Using this structure helps your story feel dynamic, logical, and engaging—highlighting challenges and the resulting actions.

The Importance of Domain Knowledge

Domain knowledge is understanding the context, nuances, and real-world systems behind your data. It helps you interpret patterns accurately and tell meaningful stories.

  • Allows you to identify which trends are significant versus noise.
  • Helps contextualize findings for specific audiences, such as transit planners or commuters.
  • Ensures your story is realistic and actionable, grounded in the system you're analyzing.
  • Example: Knowing how NYC buses are scheduled, traffic patterns, and borough characteristics can explain why certain routes slow down more than others.

Without domain knowledge, even accurate data can be misinterpreted or fail to resonate with your audience.

Key Metrics: The ACE Program

Let's start with a question for the audience:

  • What is the ACE program?
  • Do ACE violations only occur where there is a bus lane?
  • What is the purpose of the ACE program?

In analyzing the program, we focus on these key metrics:

  • Average Speed of Bus: The mean speed of a bus along a route, measured in miles per hour.
  • Wait Assessment: Time passengers wait at stops before boarding.
  • Bus Stop Time: Duration a bus spends at each stop picking up and dropping off passengers.
  • Additional Travel Time: Extra time incurred due to delays, traffic, or operational inefficiencies.
  • Customer Journey Time Performance: Total time passengers experience from start to end of their trip, including walking, waiting, and riding.

Understanding and defining these metrics helps us objectively evaluate the ACE program's effectiveness.

Feature Engineering & Custom Metrics

Sometimes the standard metrics aren't enough. We can create new metrics to better capture performance and insights.

  • Violation Density: Use polygons to calculate the density of ACE violations along a route.
  • % of Violations Not Exempt: Measure the proportion of violations that are enforceable.
  • Bus/Car Speed Ratio: A custom metric to compare bus speeds to car speeds.
    Equation: Bus/Car Speed Ratio = Average Bus Speed ÷ Average Car Speed

Framework for Visualizations

Visualizations should clearly communicate your insights. A simple framework helps:

  • Highlight the most important data points or trends.
  • Use color intentionally to guide attention.
  • Keep charts simple and uncluttered.
  • Label axes, units, and key categories clearly.
